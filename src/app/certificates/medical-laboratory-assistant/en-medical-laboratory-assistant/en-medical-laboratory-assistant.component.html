Certificate of Medical Laboratory Assistant

A Medical Laboratory Assistant is a person who, under the supervision of a medical technologist or physician, performs microscopic and bacteriologic tests of human blood, tissue, and fluids for diagnostic and research purposes.

The principal responsibilities of a Medical Laboratory Assistant are as follows:

  • Prepare specimens for analysis and ensure proper identification.
  • Perform laboratory tests and procedures under supervision.
  • Clean and sterilize laboratory equipment and glassware.
  • Maintain laboratory records and inventory of supplies.
  • Collect blood samples from patients (phlebotomy) and process them.
  • Assist medical technologists with complex laboratory procedures.
  • Ensure compliance with safety regulations and infection control protocols.
  • Perform quality control checks on equipment and reagents.
  • Communicate effectively with patients and healthcare staff.

Medical Laboratory Assistants are essential to the healthcare team, ensuring that accurate test results are available for the diagnosis and treatment of patients in hospitals, clinics, and research laboratories.

More and more employers require a professional Medical Laboratory Assistant certification.

Necessary requirements:

Graduation from High School (or equivalent), and graduation from an approved Medical Laboratory Assistant program within the last 5 years.

Or

1-year verifiable work experience as a Medical Laboratory Assistant.

Medical Laboratory Assistant Blueprint

Domains

  • I. Laboratory Safety and Quality - 30 Hours
  • II. Pre-examination (Preanalytical) Considerations - 25 Hours
  • III. Examination (Analytical) Considerations - 50 Hours
  • IV. Patient Test Management and Communications - 25 Hours
  • V. Specimen Collection and Processing - 30 Hours

I. Laboratory Safety and Quality

  • Competency 1. Adhering to OSHA safety regulations and standard precautions to prevent exposure to potential hazards.
  • Competency 2. Implementing infection control procedures including proper hand hygiene and use of PPE.
  • Competency 3. Performing quality control (QC) procedures and documenting results accurately.
  • Competency 4. Maintaining laboratory equipment integrity through calibration and preventive maintenance logging.
  • Competency 5. Properly handling and disposing of biohazardous waste and sharps.
  • Competency 6. Understanding Safety Data Sheets (SDS) and chemical safety protocols.

II. Pre-examination (Preanalytical) Considerations

  • Competency 1. Verifying patient identification and test orders prior to specimen collection.
  • Competency 2. Assessing patient preparation status (e.g., fasting) for accurate test results.
  • Competency 3. Selecting appropriate collection containers and additives for specific tests.
  • Competency 4. Recognizing factors that affect specimen integrity (e.g., hemolysis, clotting).
  • Competency 5. Transporting specimens within required timeframes and temperature conditions.

III. Examination (Analytical) Considerations

  • Competency 1. Assisting with basic clinical chemistry tests (e.g., glucose, electrolytes).
  • Competency 2. Preparing slides and performing basic hematology procedures (e.g., blood smears).
  • Competency 3. Processing microbiology specimens for culture and sensitivity testing.
  • Competency 4. Performing qualitative urinalysis and preparing urine for microscopic examination.
  • Competency 5. Conducting basic immunology and serology tests under supervision.
  • Competency 6. Operating automated laboratory analyzers and troubleshooting minor errors.

IV. Patient Test Management and Communications

  • Competency 1. Entering patient data and test requests into Laboratory Information Systems (LIS).
  • Competency 2. Communicating critical values and test status to appropriate healthcare providers.
  • Competency 3. Maintaining patient confidentiality and complying with HIPAA regulations.
  • Competency 4. Interacting professionally with patients during specimen collection procedures.
  • Competency 5. Documenting and reporting laboratory incidents or errors according to protocol.

V. Specimen Collection and Processing

  • Competency 1. Performing venipuncture using evacuated tube systems, syringes, and butterfly needles.
  • Competency 2. Conducting capillary punctures (finger/heel sticks) for point-of-care testing.
  • Competency 3. Collecting non-blood specimens such as urine, stool, and sputum samples.
  • Competency 4. Centrifuging and aliquoting specimens for testing or shipment.
  • Competency 5. Instructing patients on proper collection techniques for self-collected specimens.
