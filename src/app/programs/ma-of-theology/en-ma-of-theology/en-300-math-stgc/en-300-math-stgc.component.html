MATH-STGC-300 - Systematic Theology I: God, Trinity, and Creation

This course provides a comprehensive introduction to the core doctrines of God, the Trinity, and Creation, with a focus on theological coherence, historical development, and contemporary relevance. Students will engage in critical examination of foundational theological themes and will be equipped to articulate a systematic and biblically grounded understanding of Christian doctrine within diverse global contexts.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the essential theological concepts related to the doctrine of God, including divine attributes and the names of God.
  • Analyze the biblical and historical development of Trinitarian theology and its implications for Christian faith and practice.
  • Explore theological perspectives on the doctrine of Creation, including the relationship between Creator and creation.
  • Develop skills in critical theological reflection and systematic thinking.
  • Articulate a coherent theological vision that integrates biblical foundations, historical theology, and global Christian thought.

Main Topics

  • The nature and attributes of God in Christian theology.
  • Biblical foundations of monotheism and Trinitarian revelation.
  • Historical development of Trinitarian doctrine: Church Fathers and Councils.
  • Theological models of the Trinity and contemporary challenges.
  • The doctrine of Creation: theological, philosophical, and scientific dialogues.
  • The relationship between Creator and creation: stewardship, providence, and eschatology.
  • Implications of Trinitarian and creational theology for Christian ministry and mission.

Methodology

The course employs a blend of lectures, guided readings, theological case studies, group discussions, and analytical writing. Emphasis will be placed on theological synthesis, contextual application, and scholarly engagement with classical and contemporary sources.

Evaluation

  • Participation in class discussions and theological reflections (20%).
  • Written assignments on key doctrinal topics (25%).
  • Midterm examination covering theological concepts and historical development (25%).
  • Final integrative theological paper or project (20%).
  • Oral presentation on a selected doctrine or theological issue (10%).

Course Significance

Systematic Theology I lays the essential groundwork for advanced theological inquiry and faithful ministry. Through rigorous engagement with the doctrines of God, the Trinity, and Creation, students will deepen their understanding of the Christian faith and develop a theologically informed worldview. This course is foundational for those preparing for pastoral leadership, academic research, or theological education in diverse ministerial and cultural settings.
