MATH-HC2MAR-201 - History of Christianity II: Middle Ages and the Reformation

History of Christianity II: Middle Ages and the Reformation is a course that, through critical analysis, explores the complexities of Christianity during the Middle Ages and the transformative impact of the Reformation. Students investigate theological, social, and political shifts that continue to shape global Christianity to this day.

Course Objectives:

  • Analyze the institutional and doctrinal development of medieval Christianity.
  • Understand the role of the Church in the social, political, and cultural life of medieval Europe.
  • Examine the theological, moral, and political causes that led to the Reformation.
  • Study key figures of the Protestant Reformation and their theological contributions.
  • Explore reform movements within Catholicism (Counter-Reformation).
  • Evaluate the legacy of the Reformation in modern and global Christianity.

Main Topics:

  • Christianity and power in the Middle Ages: papacy, empire, and monasticism
  • Scholasticism and medieval theological debates
  • Ecclesiastical corruption and pre-Reformation reform movements
  • Martin Luther, John Calvin, and other key reformers
  • The Counter-Reformation and the Council of Trent
  • Cultural and social impact of the Reformation
  • Christianity in expansion: missions, colonization, and new churches
  • Continuities and ruptures between the medieval and modern Church

Methodology:

The course is delivered through virtual classes, guided readings, analysis of primary and secondary sources, online discussions, and research projects. It fosters critical thinking and historical contextualization from a global and confessionally diverse perspective.

Assessment:

  • Historical essays on key themes
  • Critical reviews of theological and historical texts
  • Participation in discussion forums
  • Oral presentations or multimedia projects
  • Final integrative project combining historical synthesis and theological reflection

Importance of the Course:

This course enables students to understand the major doctrinal, cultural, and structural changes that defined Christianity between the 5th and 16th centuries. It is essential for interpreting the roots of current denominational divisions and for understanding the development of Christian identities in a globalized world with a critical, ecumenical, and contextualized approach.
