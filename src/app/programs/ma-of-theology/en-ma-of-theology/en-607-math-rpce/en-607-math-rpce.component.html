MATH-RPCE-607 - Religious Pluralism and Christian Exclusivity

Religious Pluralism and Christian Exclusivity is a course that examines the concept of religious pluralism, understanding how the coexistence of various religious traditions impacts the Christian view of exclusivity. Through the study of foundational texts, theological debates, and church history, students will explore the tensions between the Christian affirmation of the uniqueness of Christ and the growing religious diversity in the contemporary world.

Course Objectives:

  • Understand the concept of religious pluralism and its historical development.
  • Analyze the Christian doctrine of the exclusivity of Christ in the context of religious pluralism.
  • Explore the ethical and theological implications of religious coexistence in modern societies.
  • Examine how Christians should respond to religious pluralism from a biblical and theological perspective.
  • Reflect on pastoral attitudes and Christian practices toward other religious traditions.

Main Topics:

  • Definition and historical development of religious pluralism.
  • The exclusivity of Christ: biblical and doctrinal foundations.
  • Relationship between Christianity and other religions throughout history.
  • The challenge of pluralism in modern, globalized societies.
  • Interfaith dialogue: opportunities and challenges.
  • Christian ethics in a pluralistic context.
  • The future of Christianity in the face of religious diversity.

Methodology:

The course uses case studies, critical readings of theological texts, debates, and real-life examples. Students will discuss and present how Christians can articulate their faith in contexts of religious diversity.

Assessment:

  • Theological essays on Christianity and religious pluralism.
  • Written exams on exclusivity doctrines.
  • Presentations on Christianity's role in pluralistic societies.
  • Research on interfaith dialogue and pastoral practices.

Importance of the Course:

This course is essential for understanding the theological and ethical tensions of living in a pluralistic world, preparing students for active engagement in interfaith dialogue and testimony to Christ.
