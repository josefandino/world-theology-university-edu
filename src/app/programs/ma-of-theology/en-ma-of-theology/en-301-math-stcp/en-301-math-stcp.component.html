MATH-STCP-301 - Systematic Theology II: Christology and Pneumatology

Focusing on the person and work of Christ and the Holy Spirit, this course deepens studentsâ€™ understanding of core Christian doctrines. It explores the biblical, historical, and theological foundations of Christology and Pneumatology, fostering a robust theological framework for academic research, critical reflection, and contextual pastoral engagement.

Course Objectives

  • Analyze key Christological doctrines concerning the incarnation, the dual nature of Christ, and his redemptive work.
  • Study the person and work of the Holy Spirit within biblical and historical theology.
  • Evaluate doctrinal developments emerging from Christological and Pneumatological councils.
  • Articulate a coherent and contextual theological understanding of Christ and the Spirit in the life and mission of the Church.
  • Strengthen the capacity to apply these doctrines in preaching, teaching, and Christian spirituality.

Main Topics

  • The incarnation: biblical foundations and historical theological debates.
  • The divine and human natures of Christ: hypostatic union and Christological heresies.
  • The work of Christ: redemption, reconciliation, atonement, and victory.
  • The person and work of the Holy Spirit: in creation, incarnation, the Church, and mission.
  • Pneumatology in the New Testament and patristic theology.
  • Ecumenical councils and the doctrinal development of Christology and Pneumatology.
  • The Spirit and Christian experience: sanctification, spiritual gifts, and discernment.

Methodology

The course will combine lectures, critical reading of patristic and contemporary sources, theological debates, pastoral case studies, and analytical essays. Reflective thinking and practical application of the doctrines studied will be encouraged throughout.

Evaluation

  • Participation in discussion forums and reflective activities (20%).
  • Critical essays on Christology and Pneumatology (30%).
  • Theoretical exam on doctrinal development (25%).
  • Final project: contextual theological reflection (15%).
  • Oral presentation on an assigned theological topic (10%).

Course Significance

This course is a cornerstone of systematic theological formation, equipping students to understand and communicate the foundational doctrines of Christ and the Spirit with clarity and depth. It provides the essential foundations for rigorous academic development and faithful pastoral practice marked by biblical fidelity and contextual awareness.
