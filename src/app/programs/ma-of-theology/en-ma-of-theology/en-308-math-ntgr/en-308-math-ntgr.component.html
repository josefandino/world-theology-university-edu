MATH-NTGR-308 - Natural Theology and General Revelation

Natural Theology and General Revelation is a course that explores how the existence and nature of God can be known through the observation of the natural world, without the need for special revelation. Students will study the concepts of natural theology and how general revelation, manifested through creation, human morality, and consciousness, provides evidence of God's existence and nature. This course will also address the historical debates surrounding the relationship between natural theology and biblical revelation, and how these concepts are applied in contemporary Christian thought.

Course Objectives:

  • Understand the principles of natural theology and its relationship to general revelation.
  • Study philosophical arguments for the existence of God based on the observation of the natural world.
  • Analyze how creation, morality, and human consciousness serve as means of general revelation.
  • Examine the historical tensions between natural theology and special revelation in Scripture.
  • Reflect on the implications of natural theology in contemporary Christian thought.

Main Topics:

  • The nature of general revelation: how God is made known through creation.
  • Philosophical and theological arguments for God's existence based on the observation of nature.
  • Human morality and consciousness as means of general revelation.
  • The relationship between natural theology and special revelation in the Christian tradition.
  • Contemporary debates on the role of natural theology in the modern world.

Methodology:

The course will be taught through reading key philosophical and theological texts on natural theology, in-class debates on the implications of general revelation, and critical analysis of the interactions between natural theology and special revelation. Students will be challenged to apply the concepts studied to their understanding of the Christian faith.

Assessment:

  • Essays on the relationship between natural theology and general revelation.
  • Presentations on philosophical arguments for God's existence.
  • Exams on the implications of general revelation in the Christian life.
  • Critical discussion on the tensions between natural theology and special revelation.

Importance of the Course:

This course is essential for those seeking to understand how humans can know God through creation, morality, and consciousness without the need for special revelation. Natural theology provides a pathway to integrate Christian faith with knowledge of the natural world, and remains a crucial part of contemporary theological debate.
