MATH-ECDD-203 - Ecumenical Councils and Dogmatic Development

Ecumenical Councils and Dogmatic Development is a course that studies the most important ecumenical councils in the history of the Church, their influence on the formation and evolution of Christian doctrine, and how these events have contributed to dogmatic development over the centuries. Students will explore key councils such as the Councils of Nicaea, Chalcedon, and Vatican II, and analyze how the decisions made at these councils have shaped Christian theology and practice. The course also addresses the process of dogmatic development, including the tensions between tradition and theological evolution within the Church.

Course Objectives:

  • Study key ecumenical councils in the history of the Christian Church.
  • Analyze the impact of these councils on the formation of Christian doctrine and dogmatic development.
  • Examine the dogmatic decisions of the councils and their implications for Christian practice and beliefs.
  • Investigate the historical process of theological evolution within the Church, with emphasis on the role of ecumenical councils.
  • Develop a critical understanding of doctrinal controversies and how the councils have resolved them.

Main Topics:

  • Introduction to ecumenical councils and their importance in Church history.
  • The Council of Nicaea (325 AD) and the definition of the doctrine of the Trinity.
  • The Council of Chalcedon (451 AD) and Christology.
  • The Council of Trent (1545-1563) and the Catholic Church's reform.
  • The Second Vatican Council (1962-1965) and its reforms in Catholic practice and teaching.
  • The process of dogmatic development and its relation to contemporary challenges.
  • The role of councils in resolving doctrinal controversies and ecclesial unity.

Methodology:

The course will be delivered through lectures, readings of historical documents, and case studies on ecumenical councils. Students will engage in discussions on the theological and social implications of the decisions made at the councils. Critical analysis of dogmatic debates and their impact on the Church and society will also be encouraged.

Assessment:

  • Essays on the ecumenical councils and their influence on Christian doctrine.
  • Exams on key dogmatic decisions and their impact on the Church.
  • Research projects on dogmatic development and its evolution.
  • Presentations on the councils and their relevance in modern Christianity.

Importance of the Course:

This course is essential for understanding how ecumenical councils have influenced Christian doctrine over the centuries and how dogmatic development has responded to theological and social challenges. Students will gain a deep understanding of the history of the Church and its theological evolution, enabling them to apply this knowledge in their professional lives and theological studies.
