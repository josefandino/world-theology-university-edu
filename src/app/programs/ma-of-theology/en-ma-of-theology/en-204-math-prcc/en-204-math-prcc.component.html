MATH-PRCC-204 - The Protestant Reformation and the Catholic Counter-Reformation

The Protestant Reformation and the Catholic Counter-Reformation is a course that invites students to critically examine the causes, key figures, and theological innovations of these two foundational movements of the 16th century. The course emphasizes their lasting influence on church structures, Christian theology, and the formation of global Christianity.

Course Objectives:

  • Analyze the religious, social, and political causes that gave rise to the Protestant Reformation.
  • Study the main reformers, including Martin Luther, John Calvin, and Ulrich Zwingli.
  • Explore the Catholic Church’s response through the Counter-Reformation movement.
  • Understand the theological innovations introduced by both traditions.
  • Evaluate the long-term impact on churches, liturgy, ecclesial authority, and Christian life.
  • Reflect on the legacy of the Reformation in ecumenical dialogue and contemporary Christianity.

Main Topics:

  • Historical and spiritual context of 16th-century Europe
  • Criticism of the Church and early reform movements
  • The 95 Theses and Luther’s theology
  • Calvinism and the Protestant ethic
  • The Counter-Reformation: Council of Trent and the Jesuits
  • Liturgical, doctrinal, and pastoral reforms
  • Confessional divisions and religious wars
  • Global repercussions of the Reformation and Counter-Reformation

Methodology:

The course will be delivered through virtual lectures, analysis of historical and theological sources, guided discussions, thematic research, and individual projects. A critical and historically grounded approach will be encouraged to support a contextualized understanding of reform movements.

Assessment:

  • Analytical essays on key documents from the Reformation and Counter-Reformation
  • Written exams on theological and historical content
  • Participation in critical discussion forums
  • Individual or group presentations on key figures and debates of the period
  • Final integrative project with a comparative and reflective approach

Importance of the Course:

This course is essential for understanding the formation of modern Christianity and the roots of its theological and ecclesial traditions. The Reformation and the Counter-Reformation marked a historic turning point whose consequences extend into the present, influencing the identity, practices, and mission of churches worldwide.
