This course explores, both theoretically and practically, the foundations, historical developments, and contemporary challenges of ecumenism, understood as the movement toward unity and collaboration among diverse Christian denominations. Students will analyze theological, historical, and pastoral perspectives on ecumenical dialogue and cooperation, reflecting on their importance for Christian witness in the contemporary world and learning to design interchurch collaboration initiatives.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the theological and ecclesiological foundations of the ecumenical movement.
  • Critically analyze the major historical milestones of Christian ecumenism.
  • Evaluate the contributions and challenges of interdenominational dialogues.
  • Develop skills to foster practical cooperation among diverse Christian churches.
  • Design contextualized ecumenical strategies and projects for local and global realities.
  • Foster an attitude of openness, respect, and commitment to Christian unity.

Main Topics

  • Biblical and theological foundations of ecumenism.
  • History of the ecumenical movement: from the twentieth century to the present.
  • Main ecumenical documents and organizations (e.g., World Council of Churches, bilateral dialogue documents).
  • Models of unity: full communion, reconciliation in diversity, practical cooperation.
  • Theological, cultural, and practical challenges for contemporary ecumenism.
  • Spiritual ecumenism: shared prayer and mission.
  • Case studies: successful experiences of interchurch collaboration.
  • Design of local initiatives for ecumenical cooperation.

Methodology

The course will combine virtual lectures, analysis of historical and contemporary documents, theological debates in forums, ecumenical project design workshops, case studies, and simulations of interdenominational dialogue. Experiential learning, practical engagement, and critical thinking will be strongly encouraged.

Assessment

  • Active participation in analysis forums and ecumenical simulations (20%).
  • Critical essays on the foundations and challenges of ecumenism (25%).
  • Case study analysis of interchurch cooperation (30%).
  • Final project: design of a contextualized ecumenical initiative (15%).
  • Integrative exam on ecumenical history, theology, and practice (10%).

Course Significance

This course is fundamental for preparing Christian leaders committed to unity and the shared witness of the Gospel in a fragmented world. It fosters critical and practical reflection on interchurch collaboration, equipping students to build bridges of understanding, cooperation, and common mission among diverse Christian traditions.
