This course offers a critical study of the major reforms and religious movements that emerged during the modern and contemporary eras across various faith traditions. Students will analyze the causes, processes, and consequences of reform movements, evaluating their impact on doctrinal renewal, community life, social dynamics, and global religious thought. The course addresses the diversity of religious responses to cultural, scientific, and political changes over recent centuries.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the historical, social, and theological factors that drove religious reform movements.
  • Critically analyze major reforms and their doctrinal and communal implications.
  • Evaluate the impact of modern religious movements on contemporary societies.
  • Identify common patterns and differences among reform movements in various traditions.
  • Develop a critical and contextualized perspective on processes of religious change.
  • Apply historical-religious analysis to understanding current dynamics within faith communities.

Main Topics

  • The Protestant Reformation: causes, development, and consequences.
  • The Counter-Reformation and Catholic renewal.
  • Renewal movements within modern Judaism: Reform, Conservative, and Orthodox Judaism.
  • Reforms and movements in contemporary Islam.
  • Spiritual revivals and Evangelical and Pentecostal movements.
  • Religion and modernity: challenges of secularization and pluralism.
  • Ecumenical and interreligious movements in the modern era.
  • Contemporary cases of global religious reform and transformation.

Methodology

The course will combine virtual lectures, analysis of historical and doctrinal sources, case studies on specific movements, critical discussion forums, and comparative research projects. Critical reflection, interdisciplinary analysis, and applied learning will be strongly encouraged.

Assessment

  • Active participation in analysis forums and critical debates (20%).
  • Analytical essays on reformist religious movements (25%).
  • Analysis of historical cases of religious reform (30%).
  • Final research project on a modern religious movement (15%).
  • Integrative exam on historical-religious knowledge (10%).

Course Significance

This course is fundamental for understanding the dynamics of change, resistance, and renewal within religious traditions in the contemporary world. It prepares leaders and scholars capable of critically analyzing processes of religious transformation and contributing to theological, social, and cultural reflection in contexts of rapid change and growing diversity.
