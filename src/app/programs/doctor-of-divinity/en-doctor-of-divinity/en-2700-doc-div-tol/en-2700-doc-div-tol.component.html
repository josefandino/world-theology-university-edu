DOC-DIV-TOL-2700 – Theology of Liberation

This course offers a critical analysis of Liberation Theology, exploring its emergence, theological foundations, and its relevance in addressing contemporary challenges of social justice, equity, and the transformation of oppressive structures. Students will examine the role of Christian faith in denouncing injustice, defending human rights, and building more just societies, from a contextualized and committed theological perspective.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the historical, social, and theological origins of Liberation Theology.
  • Critically analyze the fundamental concepts and theological proposals of the movement.
  • Evaluate the contemporary relevance of Liberation Theology in contexts of social and economic injustice.
  • Develop a theological reflection committed to justice, equity, and social transformation.
  • Apply principles of Liberation Theology to the analysis of current issues.
  • Foster ethical and prophetic leadership oriented toward the promotion of human rights and the dignity of the oppressed.

Main Topics

  • Historical and social context of the emergence of Liberation Theology.
  • Fundamental theological principles: preferential option for the poor, liberating praxis, and the relationship between faith and politics.
  • Analysis of key texts by liberation theologians (Gustavo Gutiérrez, Leonardo Boff, among others).
  • The relationship between theology, social justice, and human rights.
  • Internal and external critiques and debates regarding Liberation Theology.
  • Intersections with other contextual theologies: feminist, Black, and Indigenous theologies.
  • Contemporary applications of Liberation Theology in various global contexts.
  • Design of a pastoral and social action project inspired by liberating praxis.

Methodology

The course will combine virtual lectures, critical analysis of foundational texts, ethical and theological discussion forums, case studies of liberating praxis, the writing of reflective essays, and the design of social action projects. Critical thinking, ethical commitment, and practical application of theological knowledge will be strongly encouraged.

Assessment

  • Active participation in critical analysis forums (20%).
  • Analytical essays on foundational texts of Liberation Theology (25%).
  • Case study analysis on liberating praxis (30%).
  • Final project: social intervention proposal from a liberating perspective (15%).
  • Integrative exam on foundations and debates of Liberation Theology (10%).

Course Significance

This course is essential for forming critical theological leaders who are sensitive to realities of oppression and marginalization and committed to social transformation. It provides conceptual and practical tools for a contextualized theological reflection that promotes justice, equity, and human dignity in the contemporary world.
