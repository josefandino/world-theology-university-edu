This course offers a detailed analysis of the major themes of systematic theology, exploring fundamental doctrines such as God, salvation, and Christian life from a contemporary perspective and in dialogue with diverse religious traditions. Through a critical and comparative approach, students will examine classical and current formulations, integrating theological reflections that respond to the global and cultural challenges of our time.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the central themes of classical systematic theology and their contemporary development.
  • Critically analyze doctrinal formulations concerning God, salvation, and Christian life.
  • Compare theological perspectives from different traditions and cultural contexts.
  • Develop skills to construct solid, critical, and contextualized theological reflection.
  • Apply theological content to practical situations in leadership, teaching, and ministerial care.
  • Promote interreligious dialogue and respect for theological diversity in global contexts.

Main Topics

  • The Doctrine of God: attributes, Trinity, and revelation.
  • Theological anthropology: creation and human dignity.
  • Christology: the person and work of Jesus Christ.
  • Soteriology: theories of salvation and their contemporary relevance.
  • Pneumatology: the Holy Spirit and His work in the world.
  • Ecclesiology: the nature, mission, and future of the Church.
  • Escatology: Christian hope and conceptions of the ultimate end.
  • Contemporary perspectives and diversity in global theological thought.

Methodology

The course will combine virtual master lectures, theological debates, comparative analysis of doctrines, the writing of critical essays, and applied theological reflection projects. Autonomous work, analytical reading of foundational texts, and active participation in academic theological forums will be encouraged.

Assessment

  • Active participation in debates and theological forums (20%).
  • Comparative analysis of theological doctrines (25%).
  • Critical theological reflection essays (30%).
  • Final project applying theology in a specific context (15%).
  • Integrative doctrinal knowledge exam (10%).

Course Significance

This course strengthens the critical and systematic thinking necessary to address the essential themes of Christian faith in a pluralistic and ever-changing world. A deep understanding of the principal doctrines and their contextual application prepares students to exercise theological, academic, and ministerial leadership with excellence, ecumenical openness, and ethical commitment in both local and international contexts.
