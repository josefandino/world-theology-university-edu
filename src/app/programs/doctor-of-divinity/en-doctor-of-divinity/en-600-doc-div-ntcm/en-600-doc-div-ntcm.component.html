This course offers an advanced interpretation of the New Testament, addressing its emergence within the historical, cultural, political, and religious contexts of the first century. Students will explore the social dynamics, cultural influences, and theological developments that shaped the New Testament writings, applying contemporary critical methods for their analysis and interpretation. The course promotes a deep, contextualized, and relevant understanding of the texts for contemporary global theological reflection.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the historical, social, and religious background of the New Testament.
  • Analyze the main theological currents present in the New Testament texts.
  • Apply advanced exegetical methods to the study of the Gospels, epistles, and other writings.
  • Interpret New Testament texts in dialogue with their original cultural contexts.
  • Critically evaluate traditional and contemporary interpretations of the New Testament.
  • Develop relevant theological perspectives based on the contextual study of the texts.

Main Topics

  • Historical context of Second Temple Judaism and the Greco-Roman world.
  • Formation of the New Testament canon: history and criteria.
  • Critical analysis of the Gospels and their relationship to oral traditions.
  • Pauline theology and the apostolic letters: central themes and current debates.
  • Apocalyptic literature and the meaning of the Book of Revelation.
  • Contemporary methodologies for New Testament interpretation.
  • The historical Jesus and the Christ of faith: historical and theological approaches.
  • Contemporary relevance of New Testament texts in global contexts.

Methodology

The course will combine virtual lectures, analysis of primary texts, historical and theological case studies, academic forum debates, and advanced exegesis workshops. Autonomous learning will be fostered through research projects, critical reading of secondary sources, and the development of interpretative essays.

Assessment

  • Active participation in academic discussion forums (20%).
  • Critical analysis of selected New Testament passages (25%).
  • Thematic essays on New Testament contexts and theologies (30%).
  • Final research project on a contextual topic of the New Testament (15%).
  • Integrative written exam (10%).

Course Significance

This course strengthens the academic and critical competencies necessary for a solid and contextualized interpretation of the New Testament. It enables students to understand the historical and theological richness of the foundational texts of Christianity, preparing them to contribute meaningfully to theological, academic, and pastoral dialogue in global contexts, with historical sensitivity and ethical commitment.
