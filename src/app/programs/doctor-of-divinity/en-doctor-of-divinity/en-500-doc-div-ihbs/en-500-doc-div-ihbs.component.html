This course offers a detailed study of the Hebrew Bible, addressing its historical, literary, and theological contexts. Students will explore the origins, composition, and development of the Old Testament texts, analyzing their literary genres, theological traditions, and contemporary relevance. The course fosters a critical, academic, and interdisciplinary approach, integrating historical, archaeological, and hermeneutical tools for a thorough and contextualized analysis.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the historical, cultural, and religious context in which the Hebrew Bible was formed.
  • Analyze the main literary genres present in the Old Testament texts.
  • Identify the fundamental theological traditions of the Hebrew Bible and their development.
  • Apply critical methods in the study of ancient biblical texts.
  • Relate biblical texts to their archaeological and socio-historical backgrounds.
  • Develop academic interpretation skills for Old Testament texts.

Main Topics

  • Historical and cultural introduction to ancient Israel and its neighbors.
  • Formation and transmission of the Hebrew Bible texts.
  • Literary genres: historical narratives, poetry, laws, prophecy, and wisdom literature.
  • Theologies of covenant, creation, exodus, and monarchy in the Hebrew Bible.
  • Critical methodologies: tradition history, literary criticism, and redaction criticism.
  • The impact of biblical archaeology on Old Testament studies.
  • The Hebrew Bible in dialogue with the cultures of the ancient Near East.
  • Contemporary theological relevance of Old Testament texts.

Methodology

The course will combine virtual lectures, analysis of selected texts, archaeological case studies, academic discussion forums, and literary and theological interpretation workshops. Students will engage in critical readings, academic presentations, and research projects aimed at a deep and contextualized analysis of biblical texts.

Assessment

  • Participation in academic discussion forums (15%).
  • Critical analysis of biblical texts (25%).
  • Essays on historical, literary, and theological topics (30%).
  • Final research project on a topic related to the Hebrew Bible (20%).
  • Written exam integrating course knowledge (10%).

Course Significance

This course is essential for a solid understanding of the Old Testament, providing students with critical tools for the academic study of the foundational texts of the biblical tradition. By integrating history, literature, and theology, it prepares future Doctors of Divinity for informed, contextualized, and relevant analysis in their academic, ministerial, and intercultural dialogue endeavors at the global level.
