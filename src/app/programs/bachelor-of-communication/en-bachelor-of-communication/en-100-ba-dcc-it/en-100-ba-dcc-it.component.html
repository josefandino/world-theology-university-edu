This course offers an overview of the history and theory of communication, presenting its fundamental principles and emphasizing the critical role communication plays in society.

Introduction to Communication provides a comprehensive foundation in the historical, theoretical, and practical dimensions of human communication. Through the study of major theories, models, and paradigms, students will explore the development of communication as a social, cultural, and technological phenomenon. The course highlights the essential role of communication in shaping global societies, encouraging students to critically engage with its dynamics, challenges, and opportunities in the contemporary world. It is designed to prepare ethical, strategic, and adaptable professionals by establishing the essential groundwork for advanced studies in the field.

Course Objectives:

  • Understand the core concepts, theories, and models of communication.
  • Identify the key historical stages in the development of human communication.
  • Analyze the influence of communication on social, cultural, and political processes.
  • Critically assess various media and forms of communication in both historical and current contexts.
  • Apply foundational communication principles in academic and professional settings.
  • Develop a critical and ethical perspective on the role of communication in a globalized world.

Main Topics:

  • Definition and functions of communication: essential elements and basic processes.
  • History of human communication: from oral societies to the digital revolution.
  • Classical communication models: Shannon and Weaver, Lasswell, Jakobson, among others.
  • Key communication theories: functionalism, critical theory, social constructivism.
  • Mass media and their historical evolution: print, radio, television, Internet.
  • Communication in contemporary society: globalization, interculturality, and social networks.
  • Ethics and responsibility in communication: human rights, freedom of expression, and social responsibility.
  • Emerging trends in communication: artificial intelligence, digital media, and transmedia communication.

Methodology:

The course will be conducted entirely online, integrating diverse learning strategies to enhance participation and critical engagement:

  • Weekly virtual lectures led by experts in communication studies.
  • Case studies of historical and current events to bridge theory and practice.
  • Participation in academic discussion forums fostering intercultural dialogue.
  • Assigned readings of classic and contemporary communication texts.
  • Practical exercises applying theoretical principles to real-world contexts.
  • Final project focused on critical analysis of a relevant communication phenomenon.

Assessment:

  • Critical essay (25%): Reflective analysis of a communication theory or model.
  • Case study analysis (20%): Application of theoretical frameworks to a current communication issue.
  • Forum participation (15%): Thoughtful and well-supported contributions to academic discussions.
  • Written tests (20%): Individual assessments on key theoretical content.
  • Final project (20%): Research report on the impact of communication in contemporary society.

Course Significance:

Introduction to Communication serves as a foundational course within the Communication degree program, equipping students with the conceptual and analytical tools necessary to understand communicative phenomena in their historical and contemporary complexity. Its integrative and critical approach prepares students to become professionals with strategic insight, ethical sensitivity, and the adaptability required to thrive in multicultural and technological environments. This course provides a solid base for future academic and professional pursuits in areas such as journalism, corporate communication, digital media, public relations, and strategic communication consulting.
