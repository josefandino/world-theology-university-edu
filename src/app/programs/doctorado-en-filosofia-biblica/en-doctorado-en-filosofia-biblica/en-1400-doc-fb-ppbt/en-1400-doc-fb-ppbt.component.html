This course explores the intersections between political thought and biblical theological ideas, critically analyzing how key concepts such as power, law, justice, and community have been interpreted and debated through both classical and contemporary philosophical lenses. Students will investigate the impact of biblical categories on the development of political theory and current ethical-political debates, fostering critical, ethical, and context-aware reflection.

Course Objectives

  • Understand major classical and contemporary political theories in dialogue with biblical theological concepts.
  • Analyze the categories of power, law, justice, and community from both philosophical and biblical perspectives.
  • Explore the influence of the biblical tradition on the formation of Western political ideas.
  • Critically evaluate the tensions and convergences between secular political thought and biblical theology.
  • Develop critical argumentation skills on issues related to politics, ethics, and religion.
  • Foster an ethical and strategic understanding of contemporary debates on governance, rights, and social justice.

Main Topics

  • Foundations of classical political thought: Plato, Aristotle, and their biblical reception.
  • The concept of law and justice in the biblical tradition.
  • Power and authority: biblical and philosophical perspectives.
  • Community, covenant, and the common good in the Old and New Testaments.
  • The legacy of Augustine of Hippo and Thomas Aquinas in Christian political philosophy.
  • Modernity, secularization, and the reinterpretation of political power.
  • Contemporary debates: religion and politics in democratic societies.
  • Case studies: social justice and biblical principles in current public policy.

Methodology

The course will be delivered through virtual lectures, critical readings of philosophical and biblical texts, discussion seminars, political-theological case studies, and applied research projects. Emphasis will be placed on critical thinking, interdisciplinary dialogue, and active participation in inclusive academic environments.

Assessment

  • Analytical essays on political philosophy and biblical theology (30%).
  • Participation in political and theological discussion forums (20%).
  • Critical analysis of contemporary cases integrating political and religious perspectives (20%).
  • Final research project on a topic at the intersection of politics and biblical theology (30%).

Course Relevance

This course is a core component of the Doctorate in Biblical Philosophy, as it provides a critical foundation for understanding how theological ideas influence and engage with historical and current political systems. It prepares students to integrate ethical, theological, and political reflection into academic research and professional practice, promoting a responsible, inclusive, and transformative approach to contemporary global debates.
