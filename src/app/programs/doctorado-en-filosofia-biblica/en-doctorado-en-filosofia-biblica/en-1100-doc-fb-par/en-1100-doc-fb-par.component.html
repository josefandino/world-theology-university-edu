This course offers a rigorous study of philosophical conceptions of the human being in relation to religious experience, analyzing how different philosophical and theological traditions have understood the nature of the spirit, the sense of transcendence, and the cultural expressions of faith. Students will critically explore the ways in which reflection on the human condition intertwines with religious expressions, promoting an intercultural, ethical, and reflective approach.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the main philosophical conceptions of the human being in the Western tradition and their connections to religion.
  • Analyze the concepts of spirit, soul, and transcendence across various philosophical and religious traditions.
  • Examine cultural manifestations of religious experience through the lens of philosophical anthropology.
  • Critically evaluate different perspectives on transcendence and faith.
  • Explore the role of religion in shaping human and social identity.
  • Develop interdisciplinary analytical skills and critical thinking on the relationship between philosophy, anthropology, and religion.

Main Topics

  • Introduction to philosophical anthropology: key approaches and schools of thought.
  • The human being as a religious being: philosophical and theological foundations.
  • Concepts of spirit, soul, and transcendence in the history of thought.
  • Faith as an existential and cultural experience.
  • Religion and the construction of meaning in human cultures.
  • Contemporary philosophical anthropology and the religious phenomenon.
  • Contemporary debates on human nature, spirituality, and secularization.
  • Case studies: cultural expressions of faith and their philosophical interpretation.

Methodology

The course will be delivered through virtual lectures, critical analysis of philosophical and theological texts, discussion seminars, interdisciplinary case studies, and applied research projects. Critical thinking, ethical reflection, and active participation will be fostered in a collaborative academic environment.

Assessment

  • Philosophical essays on the human condition and religious experience (30%).
  • Participation in anthropological and theological discussion forums (20%).
  • Critical analysis of cultural expressions of faith (20%).
  • Final research project on philosophical anthropology and religion (30%).

Course Relevance

This course is essential to the Doctorate in Biblical Philosophy, as it deepens the understanding of human nature in its spiritual and transcendent dimension. It equips students to integrate philosophical, anthropological, and religious reflections into their academic and professional work, strengthening their intellectual and ethical leadership in multicultural and global contexts.
