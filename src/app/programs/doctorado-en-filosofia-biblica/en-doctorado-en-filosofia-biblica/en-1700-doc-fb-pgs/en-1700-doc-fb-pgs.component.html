DOC-FB-PTGS-1700 – Public Theology and Global Society

This course examines the role of theology in today’s public sphere, analyzing how religious voices contribute to ethical, political, and social debates in a pluralistic and globalized world. Through a critical and interdisciplinary lens, students will explore both the opportunities and challenges of a public theology committed to justice, human rights, peace, and the common good across diverse global contexts.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the concept and historical development of public theology in contemporary contexts.
  • Analyze the contribution of religious traditions to global ethical, political, and social discourse.
  • Critically evaluate the opportunities and risks of religious engagement in the public sphere.
  • Explore theology’s role in addressing issues such as social justice, human rights, environmental responsibility, and intercultural dialogue.
  • Develop skills in critical analysis, ethical reasoning, and public intervention from a theological perspective.
  • Foster an inclusive, pluralistic, and ethical understanding of the social responsibility of faith in globalized contexts.

Main Topics

  • Definition and foundations of public theology.
  • Theology, ethics, and social responsibility in pluralist settings.
  • Religion and the public sphere: contemporary theories and debates.
  • Public theology and human rights: convergences and tensions.
  • Religion, politics, and social justice in the globalized world.
  • Public theology and the environment: ethics of creation and sustainability.
  • Interreligious dialogue as a contribution to peace and global justice.
  • Case studies: theological interventions in recent public debates.

Methodology

The course will be conducted through virtual lectures, critical analysis of theological and social texts, case studies, academic discussion forums, and applied research projects. Active participation, critical thinking, ethical argumentation, and collaborative learning will be encouraged in an open and pluralistic academic environment.

Assessment

  • Critical essays on the role of theology in the public sphere (30%).
  • Participation in ethical, political, and social discussion forums (20%).
  • Analysis of case studies on religious interventions in the public domain (20%).
  • Final research project on public theology and global society (30%).

Course Relevance

This course is fundamental to the Doctorate in Biblical Philosophy, as it equips students to integrate theological reflection with social and political responsibility. It fosters the ability to engage with public issues from a theological standpoint in ways that are ethically grounded, contextually aware, and globally oriented.
