DOC-FB-BTEC-300 - Biblical Theology and Early Christianity

This course delves into the theological thought of the early Christian communities, exploring its historical development, biblical foundations, and philosophical influences. Through a critical and interdisciplinary approach, students will analyze the evolution of biblical theology from the early Christian era to its projection in contemporary thought, gaining insight into its impact on the formation of Christian doctrines and its relevance in current theological debates.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the historical, social, and philosophical context in which early Christianity emerged.
  • Analyze the major theological developments of the first Christian communities.
  • Identify the philosophical influences on the formation of biblical theological thought.
  • Critically examine the evolution of core doctrines from the New Testament to the Church Fathers.
  • Assess the contemporary relevance of early theological approaches in current discourse.
  • Develop research and theological argumentation skills based on historical and philosophical sources.

Main Topics

  • Historical and philosophical context of the emergence of Christianity.
  • The early Christian communities: structure, faith, and practice.
  • Early Christology: development and theological debates.
  • Influence of Jewish and Hellenistic thought on nascent Christianity.
  • Pauline theology and its legacy in biblical theology.
  • The Apostolic Fathers and the initial systematization of Christian doctrine.
  • Heresies and orthodoxy in the early centuries: philosophical and theological implications.
  • The projection of early Christianity in contemporary theological thought.

Methodology

The course will be conducted through virtual lectures, critical reading of biblical and patristic texts, comparative analysis of doctrines, participation in academic forums, and the development of research essays. Critical reflection, interdisciplinary dialogue, and autonomous learning will be encouraged, with personalized guidance from faculty members.

Assessment

  • Theological and historical analysis essays (30%).
  • Active participation in discussion forums and virtual seminars (20%).
  • Tests on critical comprehension of foundational texts (20%).
  • Final research project on a topic related to biblical theology or early Christianity (30%).
