This course is dedicated to the application of critical theory to the study of religion, analyzing how structures of power, ideology, and culture shape the interpretation, experience, and transformation of religious practice in the modern world. Through an interdisciplinary approach, students will explore how critical thinking can uncover the dynamics of domination and emancipation within contemporary religious contexts.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the foundations of critical theory and its relevance to the analysis of religious phenomena.
  • Analyze the relationship between religion, power, and ideology across historical and contemporary contexts.
  • Explore how cultural and social dynamics impact religious interpretation and practice.
  • Critically evaluate the emancipatory potential of religion in the face of oppressive systems.
  • Develop interdisciplinary analytical skills for the critical study of religion.
  • Foster an ethical, critical, and engaged perspective on religion in modern society.

Main Topics

  • Introduction to critical theory: the Frankfurt School and contemporary developments.
  • Religion, power, and society: a critical approach.
  • Ideology and religion: analyses by Marx, Adorno, and Habermas.
  • Cultural critique and deconstruction in the study of religious traditions.
  • Religion, gender, and power: a critical perspective.
  • Colonialism, postcolonialism, and religious practices.
  • Religion and emancipation: utopias, resistance, and transformation.
  • Case studies: power dynamics in contemporary religious movements.

Methodology

The course will be conducted through virtual lectures, critical readings of philosophical and sociological texts, contemporary case analyses, academic forum debates, and applied research projects. Critical thinking, interdisciplinary argumentation, and active participation in collaborative learning environments will be emphasized.

Assessment

  • Critical analysis essays on religion, power, and ideology (30%).
  • Participation in discussion forums and virtual seminars (20%).
  • Case analysis on religious dynamics and power structures (20%).
  • Final research project on critical theory applied to the study of religion (30%).

Course Relevance

This course strengthens the Doctorate in Biblical Philosophy by equipping students to engage in deep, critical, and ethical analysis of the dimensions of power and ideology within religious practices. It provides essential conceptual and methodological tools for approaching religion from a transformative perspective capable of critically engaging with the major social, political, and cultural challenges of our time.
