DOC-FB-PHST-1000 – Philosophical Hermeneutics of Sacred Texts

This course offers a critical exploration of the principal hermeneutical approaches applied to Scripture, examining how contemporary philosophy, phenomenology, and critical theory enrich and challenge traditional biblical interpretation. Students will analyze various models for understanding sacred texts, developing skills to critically interpret their meanings, contexts, and ethical projections in contemporary settings.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the foundations of philosophical hermeneutics and their application to sacred texts.
  • Analyze the contributions of contemporary philosophy, phenomenology, and critical theory to biblical interpretation.
  • Critically evaluate both traditional and contemporary approaches to biblical exegesis.
  • Develop reflective, critical, and context-sensitive interpretive skills for religious texts.
  • Explore the ethical and cultural challenges of scriptural interpretation in today's world.
  • Promote an intercultural, dialogical, and philosophically grounded reading of sacred texts.

Main Topics

  • Foundations of philosophical hermeneutics: from Schleiermacher to Gadamer.
  • Phenomenology and sacred texts: insights from Husserl and Ricoeur.
  • Critical hermeneutics and ideology critique: approaches from Paul Ricoeur and Jürgen Habermas.
  • Biblical interpretation in contemporary thought: challenges and perspectives.
  • Language, symbol, and myth in the philosophical exegesis of Scripture.
  • Hermeneutics of suspicion: Marxism, psychoanalysis, and deconstruction applied to biblical texts.
  • Ethical implications of contemporary interpretations of sacred texts.
  • Case studies: philosophical-hermeneutic analysis of key biblical passages.

Methodology

The course will be conducted through virtual lectures, critical readings of philosophical and theological texts, seminar discussions, hermeneutic case analyses, and applied research projects. Emphasis will be placed on critical thinking, philosophical argumentation, and active participation within a rigorous and inclusive academic environment.

Assessment

  • Critical essays analyzing sacred texts through a hermeneutical lens (30%).
  • Participation in philosophical and theological discussion forums (20%).
  • Case study analysis of biblical passage interpretations (20%).
  • Final research project involving applied hermeneutical interpretation of a sacred text (30%).

Course Relevance

This course is fundamental to the Doctorate in Biblical Philosophy program, as it equips students with advanced tools for the critical, profound, and ethical interpretation of sacred texts. It facilitates an interdisciplinary and contemporary approach to exegesis, strengthening academic, research, and pastoral leadership in multicultural and globalized contexts.
