This course offers a critical analysis of contemporary philosophical discussions on religion, addressing key topics such as the existence of God, religious diversity, the rationality of faith, mystical experience, and the complex interactions between science and religion. Through an interdisciplinary perspective, students will evaluate the main arguments, currents, and challenges shaping the current landscape of the philosophy of religion.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the main contemporary debates in the philosophy of religion.
  • Analyze philosophical arguments concerning the existence and nature of God.
  • Explore the philosophical implications of religious diversity and pluralism.
  • Critically assess discussions on the rationality of faith and religious experience.
  • Reflect on the dialogues and tensions between science and religion in current thought.
  • Develop skills in critical analysis, philosophical argumentation, and ethical reflection on contemporary religious issues.

Main Topics

  • The existence of God: contemporary theist, atheist, and agnostic arguments.
  • Religious diversity: exclusivism, inclusivism, and pluralism.
  • The rationality of faith: debates among faith, reason, and skepticism.
  • Mystical experience: phenomenology and epistemology of the sacred.
  • Science and religion: dialogue, conflict, and integration perspectives.
  • The problem of evil and theodicy in contemporary philosophical thought.
  • Natural religion and analytical philosophy of religion.
  • Case studies: contemporary debates on religion in the public sphere.

Methodology

The course will be delivered through virtual lectures, critical readings of contemporary philosophical texts, discussion seminars, analysis of current debates, and applied research projects. Critical thinking, rigorous argumentation, and interdisciplinary dialogue will be encouraged in a collaborative academic environment.

Assessment

  • Philosophical essays on contemporary religious debates (30%).
  • Participation in critical discussion forums and thematic seminars (20%).
  • Critical analysis of case studies and current issues (20%).
  • Final research project on a relevant philosophical debate in religion (30%).

Course Relevance

This course is essential within the Doctorate in Biblical Philosophy, as it places students at the center of the most relevant philosophical discussions on religion in today's world. It strengthens their critical analytical capacity, argumentative competence, and openness to interdisciplinary dialogue, preparing academic leaders capable of addressing the theoretical and practical challenges of a pluralistic and globalized religious environment.
