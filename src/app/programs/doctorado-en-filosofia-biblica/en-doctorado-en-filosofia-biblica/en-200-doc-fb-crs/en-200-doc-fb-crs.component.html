DOC-FB-CRS-200 - Comparative Studies of Religions

Through a critical and interdisciplinary approach, this course examines the worldâ€™s major religious traditions, highlighting their philosophical convergences and divergences. Students will explore the impact of religions on the ethical, cultural, and spiritual development of societies on a global scale, fostering a respectful and informed understanding of religious diversity and its implications for contemporary interreligious dialogue.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the historical, theological, and philosophical foundations of the major religious traditions.
  • Identify the philosophical convergences and divergences among various religions.
  • Analyze the role of religions in the formation of global ethical and cultural values.
  • Develop critical skills for comparing religious beliefs, practices, and worldviews.
  • Evaluate the challenges and opportunities of interreligious dialogue in contemporary contexts.
  • Apply an ethical, inclusive, and respectful approach to the analysis of global religious diversity.

Main Topics

  • Introduction to comparative religious studies.
  • Hinduism, Buddhism, and East Asian religions: foundations and philosophical perspectives.
  • Judaism, Christianity, and Islam: common roots and divergent developments.
  • Traditional African religions and indigenous spiritualities.
  • Religion and the construction of ethical and cultural values.
  • Modernity, secularization, and emerging spiritualities.
  • Interreligious dialogue: principles, challenges, and possibilities.
  • Case studies: religious interactions and conflicts in contemporary history.

Methodology

The course will be delivered through online lectures, comparative analysis of sacred texts and religious doctrines, case studies, online forum debates, and collaborative research projects. Active participation, critical thinking, and intercultural respect will be promoted as essential principles for autonomous and collaborative learning.

Assessment

  • Comparative analytical essays on religious traditions (30%).
  • Participation in discussion forums on ethical-religious topics (20%).
  • Written tests on comprehension and critical analysis (20%).
  • Final research project on interreligious dialogue or the cultural impact of religions (30%).

Course Relevance

This course serves as a cornerstone in the Doctorate in Biblical Philosophy program, providing students with the necessary tools to understand, analyze, and engage with global religious diversity. It promotes an ethical, critical, and inclusive perspective essential for academic, research, and professional work in multicultural and interreligious contexts, contributing to the development of more just, respectful, and dialogue-oriented societies.
