This course examines religion as a human phenomenon from a philosophical perspective, analyzing foundational experiences such as the sacred, faith, worship, and revelation. Through the thought of key contemporary phenomenologists, students will explore how religious experience is structured and its significance within human existence, fostering a deep and critical reflection on the religious dimension of life.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the theoretical foundations of phenomenology and its application to the study of religion.
  • Analyze the main phenomenological categories of religious experience: the sacred, faith, worship, and revelation.
  • Examine the contributions of major thinkers such as Husserl, Heidegger, Ricoeur, Marion, and Levinas to the philosophical study of religion.
  • Critically evaluate various manifestations of the sacred across diverse cultural contexts.
  • Develop philosophical and hermeneutical skills for analyzing contemporary religious phenomena.
  • Promote ethical and intercultural reflection on religious experience as a constitutive dimension of human existence.

Main Topics

  • Introduction to phenomenology: method and presuppositions.
  • Religion as a phenomenon: theoretical approaches.
  • The experience of the sacred in classical and contemporary phenomenology.
  • Faith, worship, and revelation: phenomenological analysis of their structures.
  • Husserl and the intentionality of religious consciousness.
  • Heidegger and the experience of being from a religious perspective.
  • Jean-Luc Marion and the phenomenology of gift and revelation.
  • Levinas: otherness, ethics, and transcendence in religious experience.

Methodology

The course will be conducted through virtual lectures, critical reading of phenomenological and theological texts, discussion seminars, phenomenological analysis of religious experiences, and applied research projects. Critical thinking, rigorous philosophical reflection, and active participation in collaborative settings will be strongly encouraged.

Assessment

  • Phenomenological analysis essays on religious experiences (30%).
  • Participation in philosophical and phenomenological discussion forums (20%).
  • Critical analysis of texts by major phenomenological thinkers (20%).
  • Final research project on a topic related to the phenomenology of religion (30%).

Course Relevance

This course is a key component of the Doctorate in Biblical Philosophy, offering a profound understanding of religion as a structuring phenomenon of human experience. It equips students to engage religious life with philosophical rigor, intercultural sensitivity, and critical openness, enhancing their academic and research capacities in global and multicultural contexts.
