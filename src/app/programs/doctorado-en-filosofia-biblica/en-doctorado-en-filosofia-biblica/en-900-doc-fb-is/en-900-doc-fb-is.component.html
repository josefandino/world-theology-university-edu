DOC-FB-IS-900 – Islamic Studies

This course offers an in-depth introduction to the philosophy, theology, and practice of Islam, from its foundational texts to its contemporary expressions. Through a critical and interdisciplinary approach, students will analyze the main currents of Islamic thought, internal ethical debates, and Islam’s impact on global social, cultural, and political dynamics, fostering a respectful and critical understanding of its internal diversity.

Course Objectives

  • Understand the philosophical, theological, and practical foundations of Islam through its texts and traditions.
  • Critically analyze the principal schools of classical and contemporary Islamic thought.
  • Explore ethical debates within Islam and their relevance to present-day contexts.
  • Examine reformist currents and their proposals for a modern Islamic discourse.
  • Evaluate the global social, cultural, and political impact of Islam.
  • Develop comparative and intercultural analytical skills in the study of religious traditions.

Main Topics

  • Origins of Islam: historical context, revelation, and early developments.
  • The Qur’an and the Sunnah: theological and normative foundations.
  • Major Islamic theological and philosophical schools.
  • Islam and ethics: contemporary debates on human rights, gender, and social justice.
  • Reformist and renewal movements in Islamic thought.
  • Islam and modernity: tensions, dialogues, and transformations.
  • Islam in a globalized world: diaspora, identity, and cultural dynamics.
  • Case studies: contemporary expressions of Islam in different world regions.

Methodology

The course will be delivered through virtual lectures, critical analysis of classical and contemporary Islamic texts, case studies, online debates, and applied research projects. Critical thinking, intercultural dialogue, and active participation will be encouraged, promoting a respectful and inclusive academic approach.

Assessment

  • Critical essays on theological, ethical, and social issues in Islam (30%).
  • Participation in discussion forums and intercultural debates (20%).
  • Case study analyses on contemporary expressions of Islam (20%).
  • Final research project on a relevant topic in Islamic Studies (30%).

Course Relevance

This course is a fundamental component of the Doctorate in Biblical Philosophy, as it enables students to acquire a deep, critical, and respectful understanding of one of the world’s most influential religious traditions. It provides analytical tools for intercultural dialogue, academic research, and ethical reflection in global contexts, fostering a perspective grounded in inclusion, mutual understanding, and ethical responsibility.
